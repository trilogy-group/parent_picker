# Parent Picker — Core Principles

These principles govern every design and product decision. When in doubt, test against this list.

## 1. Community Site

This is a community site, not a corporate dashboard. Parents are the audience, not back-end staff. The tone, visuals, and interactions should reflect that — warm, accessible, clearly built *for* the community.

## 2. Parents / community have 10x the capability and knowledge

Pulled directly from the brainlift. Our parent community has 10x the connections, capabilities, and local insight of our central team. The product should be designed to harvest that — surface the questions only locals can answer, the connections only neighbors have, the gut feel only people who live there carry.

## 3. Inspire confidence, not uncertainty and fear

Every page, every email, every status badge should leave the parent feeling more confident in Alpha and the process — not more anxious. Avoid language that sounds like things are uncertain, blocked, or at risk. Frame challenges as opportunities to contribute, not as warnings.

## 4. Empowered with actionable, impactful things to do

When a parent shows up, they should immediately see *something they can do* that visibly moves the ball. Not "thanks for your input." Specific, concrete, impactful asks. They should leave feeling they personally helped.

The highest-leverage asks are **ownership requests, not information requests.** Past failure mode: we ask soft ("do you know a zoning attorney?"), parents say yes generically, nothing actually moves. The fix: ask hard ("Will you own driving the CUP application to approval?") with a defined workstream, a single named owner, a defined outcome, and a visible status. The right parents self-select into ownership when the ask is concrete; the wrong fit gracefully passes. Soft "I can help" channels exist too — but they're the lighter tier, not the headline ask.

## 5. Educate them

Parents are smart but new to the location-selection process. Wherever we ask them to do something, we should also help them understand *why* it matters and *how* the process works. Education is part of every interaction, not a separate "About" page.

The richest teaching moments come when a site a parent liked doesn't pan out. That's when we explain — concretely — what we hit (zoning constraint, landlord dynamics, building unfit, permit timeline, etc.) and what we're doing about it. Done well, these explanations turn disappointment into informed advocacy: a parent who understands *why* 401 Congress fell through becomes a more useful partner on the next site.

## 6. Show the full pipeline

Parents should see the entire journey, not just the slice we're soliciting input on. Sites we're scoring, sites we've engaged with, sites we've signed an LOI on, and sites under buildout — all visible, all framed in a way that builds trust through transparency. Hiding stages makes the process feel opaque; showing them makes parents partners in the work.

## 7. Certainty in the destination, flexibility in the path

When things go sideways — a buildout delays, a deal falls through, zoning takes longer than expected — anchor parents on what's *certain* (their kid will have an Alpha school in this metro) and treat the bumps as expected detours, not threats. Frame delays and dead-ends as part of the process, not failures. A site that didn't work out should leave parents feeling informed and confident in the next move, not disappointed or worried.
